Water Infrastructure Funding: Legislative Priorities for 2025

An analysis of water infrastructure needs across Texas and recommendations for securing funding in the upcoming legislative session.

September 10, 2024
Byron Campbell, Senior Partner
Water Resources

Executive Summary

Texas faces critical water infrastructure challenges that require substantial investment and strategic policy approaches. This briefing provides a methodical analysis of the state's water infrastructure needs, current funding mechanisms, and legislative priorities for the upcoming 89th Legislative Session.

Our analysis indicates that while Texas has established important funding frameworks through the State Water Implementation Fund for Texas (SWIFT) and other programs, significant funding gaps remain, particularly for rural and economically disadvantaged communities. The 89th Legislature will need to address these challenges while considering the impacts of population growth, climate variability, and aging infrastructure.

Key findings from this briefing include:

  • Texas faces an estimated $80 billion in water infrastructure needs over the next 50 years
  • Current funding mechanisms are insufficient to meet projected needs, particularly for smaller communities
  • Aging infrastructure and climate variability are increasing the urgency for investment
  • Federal infrastructure funding presents significant opportunities but requires state matching and coordination
  • Regional disparities in water infrastructure capacity require tailored funding approaches

Water Infrastructure Landscape in Texas

Texas's water infrastructure encompasses a complex network of systems for water supply, treatment, distribution, wastewater management, and flood control. This infrastructure is managed by a diverse array of entities, including municipal utility districts, river authorities, groundwater conservation districts, and local governments.

Current State of Infrastructure

The condition of water infrastructure varies significantly across Texas:

  • Urban Areas: Major metropolitan regions generally have more robust systems but face capacity challenges due to rapid population growth and aging components
  • Rural Communities: Many smaller communities struggle with deteriorating infrastructure, limited technical capacity, and insufficient revenue bases
  • Border Regions: Colonias and other underserved areas along the Texas-Mexico border face particularly acute infrastructure deficiencies
  • Aging Systems: Approximately 35% of water and wastewater infrastructure in Texas is beyond its intended design life, leading to inefficiencies and reliability concerns

Key Infrastructure Needs

The 2022 State Water Plan and other assessments identify several critical infrastructure needs:

  • Water Supply Projects: New reservoirs, groundwater development, aquifer storage and recovery, and water reuse systems
  • Treatment Facilities: Upgrades to meet evolving water quality standards and address emerging contaminants
  • Distribution Systems: Replacement of aging pipes and reduction of water loss through leakage
  • Wastewater Infrastructure: Treatment plant modernization and collection system improvements
  • Flood Control: Detention basins, channel improvements, and green infrastructure for stormwater management
  • Resilience Measures: Infrastructure hardening and redundancy to withstand extreme weather events

Current Funding Mechanisms

Texas has established several funding mechanisms for water infrastructure, though significant gaps remain between available resources and identified needs.

State Funding Programs

  • State Water Implementation Fund for Texas (SWIFT): Established in 2013 with a $2 billion initial capitalization from the Economic Stabilization Fund (Rainy Day Fund) to provide low-interest loans for projects in the State Water Plan
  • Clean Water State Revolving Fund (CWSRF): Provides loans for wastewater treatment, nonpoint source pollution control, and watershed management
  • Drinking Water State Revolving Fund (DWSRF): Offers loans for drinking water infrastructure improvements
  • Texas Water Development Fund (DFund): State-backed general obligation bond program for water supply, wastewater, flood control, and drainage projects
  • Economically Distressed Areas Program (EDAP): Provides financial assistance for water and wastewater services in economically distressed areas
  • Flood Infrastructure Fund (FIF): Established in 2019 to provide grants and loans for drainage, flood mitigation, and flood control projects

Federal Funding Sources

  • Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA): Allocates approximately $2.9 billion to Texas over five years for water infrastructure
  • Water Infrastructure Finance and Innovation Act (WIFIA): Provides low-interest federal loans for water infrastructure projects
  • U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) Rural Development: Offers grants and loans for rural water and waste disposal projects
  • Community Development Block Grant (CDBG): Provides funding for water and sewer improvements in low-income communities
  • Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA): Offers hazard mitigation grants for flood control infrastructure

Local Funding Mechanisms

  • Municipal Bonds: Tax-exempt bonds issued by local governments to finance infrastructure projects
  • Impact Fees: One-time charges on new development to help fund infrastructure expansion
  • Utility Rates: User fees that support ongoing operations, maintenance, and capital improvements
  • Special Purpose Districts: Entities with taxing authority for specific infrastructure needs

Funding Gap Analysis

Despite these funding mechanisms, the Texas Water Development Board estimates a significant funding gap for water infrastructure. Current programs can address approximately 25-30% of identified needs, leaving a substantial shortfall that will require new funding approaches and increased investment from all levels of government.

Key Challenges and Opportunities

Texas faces several challenges in addressing water infrastructure needs, but also has opportunities to leverage existing strengths and emerging resources.

Challenges

  • Funding Adequacy: Current funding levels are insufficient to address the full scope of infrastructure needs
  • Regional Disparities: Significant variations in local capacity to fund and manage infrastructure projects
  • Technical Capacity: Many smaller communities lack the technical expertise to plan and implement complex infrastructure projects
  • Regulatory Complexity: Navigating multiple funding sources with different requirements creates administrative burdens
  • Climate Variability: Increasing frequency of droughts and floods requires more resilient infrastructure
  • Population Growth: Texas's rapid growth strains existing infrastructure and requires significant expansion

Opportunities

  • Federal Infrastructure Funding: Historic levels of federal investment through the IIJA and other programs
  • Technological Innovation: Emerging technologies for water efficiency, treatment, and management
  • Regional Collaboration: Increased interest in regional approaches to water infrastructure planning and implementation
  • Private Investment: Growing opportunities for public-private partnerships in water infrastructure
  • Economic Benefits: Water infrastructure investment creates jobs and supports economic development
  • Public Awareness: Recent water crises have increased public support for infrastructure investment

Legislative Priorities for the 89th Session

Based on our analysis, we recommend the following legislative priorities for addressing water infrastructure funding in the 89th Texas Legislative Session:

1. Enhance State Funding Programs

  • SWIFT Program Recapitalization: Allocate additional funds to the SWIFT program to maintain its long-term viability and expand its impact
  • EDAP Funding: Increase appropriations for the Economically Distressed Areas Program to address critical needs in underserved communities
  • Flood Infrastructure Fund: Expand the Flood Infrastructure Fund to support comprehensive flood mitigation projects
  • Rural Water Infrastructure Fund: Establish a dedicated funding source for rural water systems facing sustainability challenges

2. Maximize Federal Funding Leverage

  • State Matching Funds: Ensure adequate state matching funds to fully leverage federal infrastructure dollars
  • Technical Assistance: Provide resources to help communities navigate federal funding opportunities
  • Coordination Mechanisms: Establish formal coordination between state agencies to optimize federal funding utilization
  • Streamlined Processes: Align state and federal requirements where possible to reduce administrative burdens

3. Support Local Funding Capacity

  • Local Option Funding: Authorize additional local funding mechanisms for water infrastructure
  • Regional Project Incentives: Provide incentives for regional collaboration on water infrastructure projects
  • Rate Structure Guidance: Develop model rate structures that ensure financial sustainability while addressing affordability concerns
  • Asset Management Support: Fund programs to help utilities implement effective asset management practices

4. Address Equity and Affordability

  • Disadvantaged Community Criteria: Refine criteria for identifying disadvantaged communities to ensure resources reach those most in need
  • Affordability Programs: Establish a statewide water bill assistance program for low-income households
  • Technical Assistance: Expand technical assistance programs for small and disadvantaged communities
  • Capacity Development: Fund programs to build local technical, managerial, and financial capacity

5. Promote Innovation and Efficiency

  • Water Technology Fund: Establish a fund to support the adoption of innovative water technologies
  • Water Loss Reduction: Provide dedicated funding for water loss audits and infrastructure improvements
  • Energy Efficiency: Support programs to reduce energy consumption in water and wastewater systems
  • Alternative Water Supplies: Incentivize development of alternative water supplies, including water reuse and aquifer storage and recovery

Strategic Recommendations for Stakeholders

Effective advocacy for water infrastructure funding requires a coordinated approach from multiple stakeholders. We recommend the following strategies:

For Local Governments and Utilities

  • Develop comprehensive asset management plans to identify critical infrastructure needs
  • Engage with regional planning groups to ensure local priorities are reflected in the State Water Plan
  • Build coalitions with neighboring communities to advocate for regional projects
  • Document infrastructure challenges and their impacts on public health, economic development, and quality of life
  • Prepare "shovel-ready" projects that can quickly utilize funding when it becomes available

For Industry and Business Organizations

  • Quantify the economic impact of water infrastructure investment on business operations and growth
  • Highlight the job creation potential of water infrastructure projects
  • Engage with legislative committees on the connection between water infrastructure and economic competitiveness
  • Explore opportunities for public-private partnerships in water infrastructure development
  • Support research on innovative financing mechanisms for water infrastructure

For Environmental and Community Organizations

  • Document the environmental and public health benefits of water infrastructure investment
  • Advocate for equitable distribution of resources to underserved communities
  • Promote green infrastructure approaches that provide multiple benefits
  • Engage community members in identifying local water infrastructure priorities
  • Build diverse coalitions that demonstrate broad support for water infrastructure funding

For State Agencies

  • Streamline application processes for state funding programs
  • Provide technical assistance to communities navigating multiple funding sources
  • Develop comprehensive data on infrastructure needs and funding gaps
  • Coordinate with federal agencies to maximize funding opportunities
  • Evaluate program effectiveness and recommend improvements to the Legislature

Conclusion

Water infrastructure funding represents one of the most significant challenges and opportunities facing the 89th Texas Legislature. The decisions made in the upcoming session will have far-reaching implications for Texas's water security, public health, economic development, and environmental quality for decades to come.

By taking a methodical and transparent approach to addressing funding gaps, maximizing federal resources, supporting local capacity, ensuring equity, and promoting innovation, Texas can make substantial progress in meeting its water infrastructure needs. This will require collaboration across all levels of government, engagement from diverse stakeholders, and a long-term commitment to sustainable water infrastructure investment.
