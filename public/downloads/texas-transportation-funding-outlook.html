Texas Transportation Funding: 2025-2026 Outlook

A comprehensive analysis of transportation funding priorities for the upcoming legislative session, including potential impacts on infrastructure development and regional mobility initiatives.

October 15, 2024
Drew Campbell, President
Transportation

Executive Summary

As Texas continues to experience unprecedented population growth and economic expansion, the state's transportation infrastructure faces mounting pressure. This briefing examines the current state of transportation funding in Texas, analyzes emerging challenges, and provides strategic recommendations for the upcoming 89th Legislative Session.

Our analysis indicates that while Texas has made significant investments in transportation infrastructure over the past decade, funding gaps persist, particularly for maintenance of existing assets, rural connectivity, and multimodal transportation options. The 89th Legislature will need to address these challenges while balancing competing priorities in the state budget.

Key findings from this briefing include:

  • Current funding mechanisms are insufficient to meet projected transportation needs through 2035
  • Maintenance backlogs continue to grow, threatening the long-term viability of existing infrastructure
  • Rural and urban areas face distinct transportation challenges requiring tailored funding approaches
  • Emerging technologies and changing mobility patterns necessitate new funding frameworks
  • Federal infrastructure funding presents significant opportunities but requires state matching funds

Current Funding Landscape

Transportation funding in Texas comes from multiple sources, including state and federal fuel taxes, vehicle registration fees, Proposition 1 and Proposition 7 dedicated funds, and various financing mechanisms. The Texas Department of Transportation (TxDOT) operates with an annual budget of approximately $16.5 billion for FY 2024-2025.

Primary Funding Sources

  • State Motor Fuels Tax: $0.20 per gallon on gasoline and diesel (unchanged since 1991)
  • Federal Motor Fuels Tax: $0.184 per gallon on gasoline and $0.244 per gallon on diesel
  • Vehicle Registration Fees: Varies by county and vehicle type
  • Proposition 1 (2014): Portions of oil and gas production tax revenues
  • Proposition 7 (2015): Portions of sales tax and motor vehicle sales tax revenues
  • Texas Mobility Fund: Bond proceeds backed by various fees and taxes
  • Comprehensive Development Agreements: Public-private partnerships for specific projects
  • Federal Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA): Approximately $27 billion allocated to Texas over five years

Recent Funding Trends

While Texas has significantly increased transportation funding over the past decade through constitutional amendments and dedicated revenue streams, several concerning trends have emerged:

  • Stagnant fuel tax revenues due to improved vehicle fuel efficiency and increasing adoption of electric vehicles
  • Volatility in oil and gas production tax revenues affecting Proposition 1 allocations
  • Inflation in construction costs outpacing revenue growth
  • Increasing maintenance needs for aging infrastructure
  • Growing demand for new capacity in high-growth areas

Funding Challenges and Gaps

Despite recent funding increases, Texas faces significant transportation funding challenges that will require legislative attention in the upcoming session.

Maintenance Backlog

TxDOT estimates a maintenance backlog of approximately $5 billion for existing infrastructure. This includes bridge repairs, pavement rehabilitation, and safety improvements. Deferred maintenance ultimately leads to higher costs and reduced system performance.

Urban Congestion

Texas's major metropolitan areas continue to experience severe congestion, with associated economic costs estimated at $14.9 billion annually. While recent investments have targeted congestion relief, funding for comprehensive solutions remains insufficient.

Rural Connectivity

Rural transportation networks face unique challenges, including deteriorating farm-to-market roads, limited transit options, and safety concerns. These areas often struggle to compete for limited transportation dollars despite their economic importance.

Multimodal Transportation

Current funding mechanisms heavily favor highway infrastructure, with limited resources for transit, bicycle, and pedestrian facilities. As mobility patterns evolve, funding frameworks must adapt to support diverse transportation options.

Resilience and Sustainability

Texas's transportation infrastructure faces increasing threats from extreme weather events and climate change. Additional funding is needed for resilience measures, including improved drainage, elevated roadways in flood-prone areas, and more durable materials.

Funding Gap Analysis

The Texas A&M Transportation Institute estimates that Texas needs approximately $20-25 billion annually to maintain current conditions and address growth—significantly more than current funding levels. Without additional revenue sources, this gap will continue to widen, resulting in deteriorating infrastructure and increased congestion.

Federal Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) Impact

The 2021 Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act represents a historic federal investment in transportation infrastructure, with Texas receiving approximately $27 billion over five years for highways and bridges. While this funding provides significant opportunities, it also presents challenges:

Opportunities

  • Accelerated funding for critical projects
  • Dedicated funding for bridge rehabilitation and replacement
  • New programs for resilience, electric vehicle charging infrastructure, and safety improvements
  • Competitive grant opportunities for transformative projects

Challenges

  • State matching requirements (typically 20%) for federal funds
  • Limited flexibility in how funds can be used
  • Increased administrative burden for project delivery
  • Temporary nature of funding increase

The 89th Legislature will need to ensure that Texas can fully leverage IIJA funding by providing adequate matching funds and streamlining project delivery processes.

Key Legislative Considerations

As the Legislature evaluates transportation funding options, several key considerations will influence the debate:

  • Revenue Adequacy: Will the proposed funding mechanism generate sufficient revenue to address identified needs?
  • Sustainability: Is the funding source stable and growing over time?
  • Equity: Does the funding approach distribute costs fairly among users and beneficiaries?
  • Administrative Efficiency: Can the funding mechanism be implemented without excessive administrative costs?
  • Political Feasibility: Is there sufficient political support to enact the proposed changes?

Strategic Recommendations

Based on our analysis, we recommend the following strategies for addressing transportation funding challenges in the 89th Legislative Session:

1. Diversify Revenue Sources

Texas should reduce its reliance on motor fuels taxes by developing a more diverse portfolio of transportation revenue sources. This could include:

  • Implementing an annual fee for electric and hybrid vehicles
  • Exploring mileage-based user fees through a voluntary pilot program
  • Adjusting vehicle registration fees based on vehicle weight and impact on infrastructure

2. Maximize Federal Funding

To fully leverage federal infrastructure funding, Texas should:

  • Ensure adequate state matching funds are available
  • Develop a strategic approach to competitive grant programs
  • Streamline project delivery processes to accelerate implementation

3. Prioritize System Preservation

Addressing the maintenance backlog should be a top priority to prevent more costly repairs in the future. This includes:

  • Establishing dedicated funding for maintenance and rehabilitation
  • Implementing performance-based asset management
  • Investing in technologies that extend infrastructure lifespan

4. Enable Regional Solutions

Different regions of Texas face distinct transportation challenges that require tailored approaches:

  • Authorize local option funding mechanisms for metropolitan areas
  • Develop targeted programs for rural transportation needs
  • Support regional coordination and planning

5. Embrace Innovation

Emerging technologies and approaches can help stretch limited transportation dollars:

  • Invest in intelligent transportation systems to optimize existing capacity
  • Support research and development of cost-effective construction methods
  • Develop funding frameworks that accommodate evolving mobility patterns

Conclusion

Transportation funding will be a critical issue for the 89th Texas Legislature as the state continues to grow and infrastructure needs mount. While recent funding increases have provided temporary relief, long-term challenges remain, particularly as traditional revenue sources like the motor fuels tax become less sustainable.

The upcoming legislative session presents an opportunity to develop a more diverse, sustainable, and equitable transportation funding framework that can support Texas's economic vitality and quality of life for decades to come. By taking a proactive approach to addressing funding challenges now, Texas can avoid more costly and disruptive solutions in the future.
