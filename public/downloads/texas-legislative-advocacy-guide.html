How to Get the Most Out of Your Texas Legislative Advocacy: The Insider's Playbook

By Drew Campbell & Byron Campbell, Capitol Insights

Introduction: The $412 Million Case Study

"That request isn't in our budget for this session."

I'll never forget those words from the Transportation Committee Chair on February 12, 2021.

His tone was polite but firm—the voice of someone who'd made his decision months ago.

I nodded, thanked him for his time, and stood to leave. The regional transportation authority executives with me were visibly deflated. Their $500 million funding request had been effectively denied with a single sentence.

As we walked out of the committee room, one of the executives turned to me.

"So that's it?" he asked. "Two years of planning down the drain?"

"Not necessarily," I replied. "But we need to completely rethink our approach. What if I showed you how we could secure over 80% of that funding in the next session?"

Twenty-six months later, the Governor signed a transportation bill that included $412 million for that exact project.

The difference wasn't the request itself. It wasn't even the economic case, which had been sound from the beginning.

The difference was our application of what we call the Legislative Influence Matrix—a systematic approach to advocacy based on strategic timing, relationship building, and coalition development.

In this guide, I'll share exactly how we did it, and how you can apply these same principles to your own legislative priorities—regardless of your industry, size, or current political connections.

This isn't theory. It's the methodology we've refined over four decades helping organizations secure billions in funding and navigate the complex legislative landscape in Texas.

Let's begin with the timeline most organizations get completely wrong...

PART ONE: The Legislative Timeline Most Organizations Miss

The 24-Month Legislative Cycle No One Tells You About

Most people understand that the Texas Legislature meets every two years for 140 days starting in January of odd-numbered years.

What they miss is that the real legislative work—the work that determines which bills pass and which die, which priorities receive funding and which go unfunded—happens during the other 590 days of the biennium.

Here's the reality: By the time a bill is filed in January, 80% of the work that will determine its fate has already been done.

The organizations that consistently succeed in the legislative arena understand this. They approach legislative advocacy not as a seasonal sprint, but as a continuous cycle:

Phase 1: Post-Session Assessment (Months 1-2)

June-July after session

  • Analyze results from the previous session
  • Identify emerging issues and threats
  • Assess relationship strengths and gaps
  • Begin developing strategic priorities

Phase 2: Relationship Building (Months 3-9)

August-February after session

  • Host facility tours for lawmakers
  • Attend fundraisers and community events
  • Build connections with committee staff
  • Begin educating on priority issues

Phase 3: Interim Committee Engagement (Months 10-15)

March-August before session

  • Testify at interim committee hearings
  • Provide research and data to committee staff
  • Draft potential legislative language
  • Refine messaging based on committee feedback

Phase 4: Pre-Session Positioning (Months 16-18)

September-December before session

  • Finalize legislative priorities (maximum of 3)
  • Solidify champion relationships
  • Build supporting coalitions
  • Prepare collateral materials

Phase 5: Active Session Advocacy (Months 19-24)

January-May of session

  • Execute strategic advocacy plan
  • Activate established relationships
  • Monitor and respond to developments
  • Prepare for implementation

This timeline isn't arbitrary. It's based on how the Texas Legislature actually works.

When committee chairs are developing interim charges, when staff are researching issues, when lawmakers are forming their opinions—that's when effective advocacy happens.

CASE STUDY: How We Secured $412M in Transportation Funding

In 2022, one of our regional transportation clients faced a critical funding shortfall. Major corridors needed expansion, but their projects were far down TxDOT's priority list.

Instead of waiting until the 2023 session to make their case, we:

  1. Identified key decision-makers in June 2022 (18 months before session)
  2. Built relationships with Transportation Committee members and staff throughout summer and fall
  3. Hosted site visits for lawmakers to see congestion firsthand
  4. Testified at interim hearings in November
  5. Developed county-specific economic impact data to demonstrate broader benefits
  6. Built a coalition with chambers of commerce and economic development entities
  7. Refined our strategy based on interim committee feedback
  8. Activated relationships when the session began

The result? $412 million in additional funding—even as other priorities were deferred.

This didn't happen because we had special access or influence. It happened because we started 18 months before the session began, not 18 days.

PART TWO: The Three Levels of Influence Most Organizations Ignore

Why Staff Relationships Matter More Than Most Organizations Realize

When most people think about legislative advocacy, they picture meetings with lawmakers. While those relationships are important, they're only part of the equation.

The Texas Capitol operates on three distinct levels of influence:

Level 1: Legislators

The elected officials who ultimately vote on bills. They're the most visible, but often have the least time for in-depth understanding of specific issues.

Level 2: Senior Staff

Chiefs of staff, committee directors, and policy advisors who shape their bosses' priorities and positions. They're the primary information gatekeepers.

Level 3: Subject Matter Experts

Agency officials, committee clerks, and Legislative Council attorneys who draft bill language and provide technical expertise.

The most effective advocacy engages all three levels.

Consider this:

  • The average legislator serves on 2-3 committees
  • Each committee considers dozens of bills per session
  • Legislators also manage constituent services, attend events, and fundraise

It's physically impossible for them to become experts on every issue.

So who do they rely on? Their staff.

In our experience, relationships with senior staff members and subject matter experts often prove more valuable than relationships with the legislators themselves.

CASE STUDY: How a Staff Relationship Saved $28M in Water Infrastructure Funding

In 2021, one of our water utility clients was at risk of losing crucial infrastructure funding when their project was inadvertently excluded from the Committee Substitute for SB 8.

The hearing was scheduled for the next day, and the committee chair wasn't accepting meetings.

But because we had built a relationship with the committee director during the interim, we were able to:

  1. Get a 15-minute call to explain the issue
  2. Provide specific language for an amendment
  3. Have the amendment added to the committee substitute

The chairman never even knew there was a problem. His trusted staff member simply took care of it based on a relationship we had built months earlier.

The result? $28 million in water infrastructure funding preserved with a single phone call—because we had invested in staff relationships.

The Staff Relationship Blueprint

Here's how we approach staff relationships for our clients:

  1. Identify the right staff members
    • Committee directors and policy analysts
    • Chiefs of staff for committee members
    • Legislative Council attorneys assigned to your issue area
    • Agency officials who will implement legislation
  2. Provide genuine value
    • Become a reliable source of accurate, balanced information
    • Respond promptly to information requests
    • Provide research and data that makes their jobs easier
    • Respect their time and expertise
  3. Build personal connections
    • Remember important details about their backgrounds and interests
    • Recognize their contributions (which are often overlooked)
    • Maintain contact during the interim, not just during session
    • Treat them as important partners, not stepping stones to their bosses
  4. Position yourself as a resource
    • When policy questions arise in your area, be the first call they make
    • Offer context and background beyond your specific asks
    • Connect them with other experts when appropriate
    • Support their professional development

Remember: Legislative staff often have longer tenures than the legislators they serve. A key staff relationship can pay dividends across multiple sessions.

PART THREE: The Issue Framing Matrix—Turning Technical Issues into Legislative Priorities

How to Transform Technical Expertise into Compelling Policy Arguments

One of the biggest mistakes organizations make is presenting their issues exactly as they understand them internally.

What makes perfect sense to industry experts often falls flat with legislators who must balance hundreds of competing priorities.

After decades helping clients secure legislative wins, we've developed the Issue Framing Matrix—a systematic approach to positioning technical issues in ways that resonate with lawmakers:

Technical Issue Regional Impact Economic Significance Constituent Benefit Strategic Narrative
What the issue means to your industry How it affects the lawmaker's district Jobs, investment, tax base implications How it improves constituents' lives The compelling story that ties it all together

Let's see this matrix in action:

CASE STUDY: Reframing Water Infrastructure to Secure Funding

In 2019, a coalition of municipal water authorities needed substantial funding for aging infrastructure. Initially, they presented their case in highly technical terms:

Original Framing (Technical): "Municipal water systems require $1.2 billion in capital improvements to address deteriorating distribution systems and outdated treatment facilities."

This generated little interest from legislators outside the Natural Resources Committee.

We helped them reframe the issue using our matrix:

Regional Impact: "Water systems in 157 counties, including 12 in your district, face critical failures in the next decade without these improvements."

Economic Significance: "These projects will create 14,000 direct jobs and protect $87 billion in economic activity dependent on reliable water service."

Constituent Benefit: "Families will see fewer service disruptions, stable water rates, and improved water quality for their children."

Strategic Narrative: "Texas' economic miracle depends on reliable infrastructure. Water is the foundation of our continued growth and prosperity."

The result? A $900 million water infrastructure package that initially had little support became a bipartisan priority.

The Four Questions Every Effective Issue Frame Answers

When developing your legislative messaging, ensure it answers these four questions that every lawmaker and staff member is asking:

  1. Why should I care about this issue now? (Urgency)
  2. How does this affect my district specifically? (Relevance)
  3. What's the broader impact on Texas? (Significance)
  4. How can I explain this to constituents in one sentence? (Simplicity)

If your messaging doesn't clearly answer all four questions, legislators won't prioritize your issue—no matter how important it is to your organization.

PART FOUR: The Coalition-Building Strategy That Amplifies Your Voice

How to Transform Your Single Voice into a Regional Priority

In the competitive world of legislative advocacy, solo voices rarely break through the noise.

This is especially true for:

  • Regional transportation initiatives
  • Infrastructure funding requests
  • Water resource issues
  • Economic development priorities

The organizations that consistently succeed build strategic coalitions that amplify their influence.

But not all coalitions are created equal.

The Three Types of Legislative Coalitions

Type 1: Industry Coalitions

Organizations within the same sector advocating for shared interests.

Example: Multiple water authorities seeking infrastructure funding

Strength: Deep subject matter expertise

Weakness: Limited geographic influence

Type 2: Regional Coalitions

Organizations across sectors in the same geographic area.

Example: Dallas Regional Mobility Coalition combining cities, counties, and transportation agencies

Strength: Strong regional influence

Weakness: May compete with other regional priorities

Type 3: Cross-Sector Impact Coalitions

Organizations united by a specific outcome rather than industry or geography.

Example: A coalition of manufacturers, community colleges, economic development corporations, and municipalities supporting workforce development

Strength: Demonstrates broad-based impact

Weakness: Requires more coordination

The most effective approach often combines elements of all three.

CASE STUDY: The North Texas Commission's Coalition Strategy

When the North Texas Commission needed to secure critical transportation funding, they faced multiple challenges:

  • Competition from other metropolitan areas
  • A tight state budget
  • Limited awareness of regional needs

Rather than advocating alone, they built a coalition that included:

  • 5 county governments
  • 12 municipalities
  • 3 chambers of commerce
  • 7 major employers
  • 2 universities
  • Multiple transportation agencies

This coalition approach allowed them to:

  1. Demonstrate widespread support across constituencies
  2. Present unified priorities rather than competing requests
  3. Leverage multiple relationships with key lawmakers
  4. Speak with a single, powerful voice

The result? Funding for three major priority projects that might have otherwise gone unfunded.

The Coalition Building Roadmap

Here's how we help clients build effective coalitions:

  1. Identify potential coalition partners
    • Who benefits from your success?
    • Who faces similar challenges?
    • Who has complementary relationships?
    • Who brings additional credibility?
  2. Develop a unifying framework
    • Create a shared identity and purpose
    • Establish clear, achievable objectives
    • Develop governance structure and process
    • Build consensus on messaging
  3. Leverage collective relationships
    • Map existing relationships with decision-makers
    • Identify gaps and develop strategies to fill them
    • Coordinate outreach across the coalition
    • Present a unified front to lawmakers
  4. Maintain coalition cohesion
    • Regular communication to all members
    • Quick wins to demonstrate value
    • Recognition of all contributors
    • Clear process for handling disagreements

Remember: A well-structured coalition doesn't just amplify your message—it transforms your issue from a special interest into a broad public concern.

PART FIVE: Executing Effectively During the Legislative Session

How to Maximize Impact When the Legislature Convenes

If you've followed the approach outlined in the previous sections, you'll enter the legislative session with significant advantages:

  • Established relationships with key decision-makers
  • Clear, compelling framing of your priorities
  • A supportive coalition amplifying your message
  • Feedback from interim hearings to refine your approach

Now it's time to execute effectively during the intense 140-day session.

The Seven Session Execution Principles

  1. Focus on your top three priorities
    Attempting to influence too many bills dilutes your effectiveness. Identify your absolute top priorities and concentrate your resources there.
  2. Be responsive, not just proactive
    The legislative environment changes rapidly. Monitor developments daily and be prepared to adjust your strategy accordingly.
  3. Provide solutions, not just problems
    Lawmakers appreciate stakeholders who bring practical solutions, not just complaints. Always have amendment language ready.
  4. Respect the process
    Understanding procedural rules and deadlines is essential. A technically perfect bill that misses a key deadline is still a failed bill.
  5. Maintain coalition discipline
    Ensure all coalition members are consistently on message. Contradictory positions from supposed allies can fatally undermine your efforts.
  6. Support your champions
    Provide timely information, talking points, and backup to the legislators carrying your priorities. Make their job as easy as possible.
  7. Be gracious, even in defeat
    The relationships you build this session will determine your success next session. Maintain professionalism regardless of outcomes.

CASE STUDY: Defeating Harmful Regulation Through Effective Execution

In 2023, one of our clients faced potentially devastating regulatory legislation that had surprising momentum early in the session.

Because we had built relationships during the interim and developed a solid coalition, we were able to:

  1. Get early intelligence about the bill's progress
  2. Meet with key committee members before the hearing
  3. Mobilize coalition partners to testify
  4. Provide specific amendment language to address legitimate concerns
  5. Work with the author to find acceptable compromise

The result? A bill that could have crippled an entire industry was amended to address its stated purpose while protecting our client's interests.

The Critical Path Timeline for the 140-Day Session

Understanding the rhythms of the legislative session is essential for timing your advocacy efforts:

Days 1-30: Organization Phase

  • Committee assignments announced
  • Initial bill filings accelerate
  • Relationships activated
  • Position papers distributed

Days 31-60: Committee Phase I

  • Priority bills receive hearings
  • Initial testimony delivered
  • Amendments negotiated
  • Coalition advocacy intensifies

Days 61-90: Committee Phase II

  • Bills reported from committee
  • Calendar strategies implemented
  • Floor advocacy begins
  • Potential roadblocks identified

Days 91-120: Floor Action Phase

  • House and Senate floor debates
  • Amendment strategies executed
  • Conference committee preparation
  • Potential procedural challenges

Days 121-140: Endgame Phase

  • Conference committee negotiations
  • Final passage secured
  • Potential veto issues addressed
  • Implementation planning begins

The most successful organizations understand exactly where they need to focus at each phase of the session—and have the relationships in place to execute effectively.

Conclusion: The Five Principles of Transformational Legislative Advocacy

After four decades helping organizations navigate the Texas legislative process, we've distilled our approach into five core principles:

1. Begin with the timeline in mind

Effective advocacy starts at least 18 months before session, not when bills are filed.

2. Build relationships at all levels

Legislators matter, but staff relationships often determine success or failure.

3. Frame issues for maximum resonance

Technical expertise must be translated into compelling policy narratives.

4. Amplify through strategic coalitions

Transform your single voice into a broad-based priority.

5. Execute with discipline and adaptability

Focus relentlessly on priorities while remaining responsive to changing circumstances.

We've seen these principles work across industries, issues, and political environments. They're not theoretical concepts—they're practical approaches that consistently deliver results.

The organizations that thrive in the legislative arena aren't necessarily the largest or most politically connected. They're the ones that approach advocacy strategically, build relationships systematically, and execute flawlessly.

Whether you're seeking funding, fighting harmful legislation, or advancing policy priorities, these principles will help you maximize the return on your legislative advocacy investment.
